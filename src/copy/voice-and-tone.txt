0. CORE IDENTITY
----------------
SSA is not a productivity app.

SSA is a **household steward** helping a **householder** see and hold:

- cycles and seasons,
- storehouse and provision,
- yields from garden and animals,
- preservation, cleaning, textiles, maintenance,
- feasts, mutual aid, and enterprise,
- long-term household planning.

The voice should always feel like a wise, calm, practical steward:
- grounded in the land and seasons,
- curious about rhythms and yields,
- respectful of the householder’s authority.

Avoid language that sounds like a factory, corporate dashboard, or gamified app.


1. VOICE PRINCIPLES
-------------------

1.1 Steady and grounded
- Speak as if you are standing in the kitchen, garden, or storeroom with the householder.
- Use calm, measured language.
- Prefer short, concrete sentences over jargon.

Example:
- Prefer: “This rhythm looks heavy for this season. You may want to simplify.”
- Avoid: “Your workload is too high. Optimize your tasks for efficiency.”

1.2 Stewardship, not command-and-control
- SSA does not boss the householder.
- SSA offers reflections, suggestions, and gentle warnings.

Language pattern:
- “You may want to…”
- “This could be a good moment to…”
- “A gentle way to begin is…”

Avoid:
- “You must…”
- “You should always…”
- “The system requires you to…”

1.3 Season- and cycle-aware
- Tie guidance back to:
  - current or upcoming season,
  - current rhythms and storehouse posture,
  - yields and feast windows.

Example:
- “Because this is a lean season, smaller preservation flows may serve you better.”
- “A feast is approaching; it may be wise to check grain and oil in the storehouse.”

1.4 Honor the householder
- Assume the user is intelligent and capable.
- SSA provides clarity and structure, not lectures.

Example:
- “You’ve kept this rhythm for several weeks. You may be ready to deepen it.”
- NOT: “You’ve finally completed enough tasks to level up.”


2. TONE PRINCIPLES
------------------

2.1 Warm, not sentimental
- Warm: “gentle”, “steady”, “quietly building”.
- Avoid sugary or overly emotional language.

2.2 Plain speech with subtle Israelite / agrarian flavor
- Use words that echo:
  - land, storehouse, grain, oil, seasons, feasts.
- Do not quote verses or preach.
- Keep references practical and lived.

Example:
- “This preservation run will help your storehouse carry you through the next season.”
- “Your garden beds are speaking this week; yields are higher than usual.”

2.3 Encouraging but honest
- Celebrate steady rhythms and good yields.
- Warn clearly about strain or drift.

Examples:
- “Your storehouse looks lean in this category. A small provisioning cycle may be helpful.”
- “This rhythm has been quiet for a while. If it’s no longer needed, you can let it rest.”


3. KEY VOCABULARY
-----------------

3.1 Core words we intentionally use
- cycle
- season
- storehouse
- provision / provisions
- yield / yields
- rhythm
- flow
- feast
- householder
- household
- preservation
- harvest
- lean season / rich season
- mutual aid
- enterprise (household enterprise)

Example replacements:
- Instead of “project”: use “flow”, “cycle”, or “plan”.
- Instead of “schedule”: use “rhythm” or “pattern”.
- Instead of “resource”: use “provision”, “storehouse entry”, or “item”.

3.2 Words to minimize (or only use in “avoid” lists)
- task
- todo
- module
- generic item
- productivity
- workload
- user story
- sprint

If they must appear (e.g., in old copy), they should be replaced or rephrased
in new UI with household language.

Example:
- BAD: “Add a new task.”
- BETTER: “Add a new rhythm for this space.”
- SIMPLE: “Add a new flow.”


4. PATTERNS FOR UI TEXT
-----------------------

4.1 Titles
- Short, concrete, stewardship-oriented.
- Name the household area, then the type of help.

Patterns:
- “[Domain] Rhythm”
- “[Domain] Flow”
- “[Thing] & Provision”
- “[Domain] Snapshot”

Examples:
- “Cooking & Meal Rhythm”
- “Storehouse Snapshot & Planning”
- “Garden & Animals – Yields in Motion”
- “Feast & Gathering Horizon”

4.2 Subtitles
- Explain what this view helps with.
- Connect to season, storehouse, or rhythms.

Examples:
- “See how your grains, preserves, and daily staples are holding through the season.”
- “Cooking, cleaning, garden, animals, and small maintenance flows in one glance.”

4.3 Buttons and CTAs
- Focus on starting or adjusting rhythms and flows, not “managing tasks”.

Examples:
- “Start this rhythm now”
- “Open Storehouse Planner”
- “Plan a Preservation Session”
- “Begin a Gentle Cleaning Cycle”
- “Log a New Planting or Yield”

Avoid:
- “Create task”
- “Manage items”
- “Optimize workload”


5. ERROR, WARNING, AND INSIGHT LANGUAGE
---------------------------------------

5.1 Insights
- Insights are reflections, not orders.
- They speak in terms of rhythms, seasons, and storehouse posture.

Patterns:
- “This rhythm looks…”
- “Your storehouse appears…”
- “This season may be a good time to…”
- “A gentle next step could be…”

Examples:
- “Your storehouse is leaning in this category. A small provisioning cycle may help.”
- “Your garden rhythm is strong; you may be ready to deepen preservation this season.”

5.2 Warnings / Alerts
- Be clear and specific, but not harsh.
- Connect risk to future seasons or feasts.

Patterns:
- “This may draw heavily from…”
- “This may not align with the current season.”
- “This yield may pass without being preserved.”

Examples:
- “This pattern may be drawing too heavily from the storehouse.”
- “A feast is approaching; storehouse levels look tight in a few staples.”

5.3 Errors
- Focus on what went wrong and how to recover.
- Avoid blame.

Examples:
- “Something interrupted this flow. Please try again.”
- “SSA couldn’t save this rhythm right now. Check your connection and try again.”
- “A setting is missing. Fill in the highlighted fields so this flow can be recorded.”


6. PERSON REFERENCE & PRONOUNS
------------------------------

6.1 Householder
- Use “householder” when referring to the responsible person.
- Use “household” when referring to the web of people, spaces, and rhythms.

Examples:
- “The householder can adjust this rhythm at any time.”
- “This rhythm supports the whole household, not just one room.”

6.2 Second-person “you”
- Use “you” to speak directly to the householder.
- Do not use “the user” in UI-facing copy.

Examples:
- “You can begin with one simple rhythm.”
- “You may want to review your storehouse before this feast.”


7. SMALL DETAILS THAT MATTER
----------------------------

7.1 Time language
- Prefer “rhythm” and “pattern” over “schedule”.
- Use simple time phrases when needed: “mornings”, “before sunset”, “after harvest”.

Bad:
- “Schedule your tasks for this time slot.”
Good:
- “This rhythm usually happens in the evenings.”

7.2 Data and numbers
- Connect numbers to meaningful household concepts: jars, meals, weeks of coverage.

Example:
- “This storehouse category holds about three weeks of meals at your current rhythm.”
- “You preserved 12 jars from this harvest; that’s a steady yield for the coming season.”

7.3 Progress & mastery
- Avoid gamified “levels” and “points.”
- Use language of steady growth and stages of stewardship.

Examples:
- “A steady rhythm is forming here.”
- “You have carried this rhythm through several seasons.”
- “You may be ready to host a larger feast or share your provision with others.”


8. TOOL MODE VS STEWARDSHIP MODE
--------------------------------

8.1 TOOL MODE
- Emphasize simplicity and local usefulness.
- Acknowledge that this part can later join the bigger web.

Patterns:
- “This page works on its own.”
- “You are shaping one household rhythm.”
- “SSA can link this flow with your seasons and storehouse when you’re ready.”

8.2 STEWARDSHIP MODE
- Emphasize connectedness and gentle oversight.
- Describe how domains speak to each other.

Patterns:
- “This dashboard weaves your cooking, storehouse, garden, preservation, cleaning,
  and feasts into one stewardship view.”
- “These panels show how your cycles, seasons, and yields are working together.”


9. DO & DON’T CHEATSHEET
------------------------

DO:
- Speak of cycles, seasons, rhythms, flows, yields, provisions, feasts.
- Ground advice in concrete household realities.
- Respect the householder’s pace and capacity.
- Use calm, plain sentences.
- Tie suggestions to season, storehouse posture, and long-term planning.

DON’T:
- Say “task”, “todo”, “module”, “workload”, “optimize”.
- Sound like a corporate productivity dashboard.
- Scold or shame.
- Over-spiritualize or sermonize.
- Use jargon that makes the householder feel small or confused.


10. SAMPLE REWRITES
-------------------

Example 1:
- OLD: “No tasks have been created. Click ‘Add Task’ to get started.”
- NEW: “No rhythms are recorded here yet. Begin by naming one small flow you already do.”

Example 2:
- OLD: “Your pantry items are low. Add a shopping task.”
- NEW: “Your storehouse is leaning in a few staples. A small provisioning cycle may be helpful.”

Example 3:
- OLD: “Optimize your chores to reduce workload.”
- NEW: “Shape gentle cleaning rhythms that guard rest and keep shared spaces steady.”

Example 4:
- OLD: “View your modules and manage dependencies.”
- NEW: “See how your flows connect: garden → preservation → storehouse → meals.”

Example 5:
- OLD: “Set a reminder for this event.”
- NEW: “Mark this rhythm so SSA can help you remember when the season comes.”
